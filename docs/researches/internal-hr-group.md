## 2. Agentic Organization Content Brief

If BCE approves the "agentic organization" module for the main deck, **here's what to brief in**:

### 2.1 **What Is an Agentic Organization?**

An agentic organization isn't a new department or a committee. It's a **way of working** where:

- **Humans specify** what needs to happen (requirements, business logic, constraints)
- **AI generates** bulk work (code, analysis, documentation, iteration)
- **Humans verify** quality and fit (review, test, validate before shipping)

This differs radically from the old model where humans did 70% execution (writing, building, producing) and only 30% planning/review. The agentic model inverts this.

**Real impact**: 25–55% productivity gain in controlled studies. 78% of organizations adopt AI, but only 6% implement it *properly*. The gap kills adoption.

### 2.2 **Why Organizations Need It**

**Problem**: Without structure, AI tools become chaos.
- Teams pick different tools (fragmentation).
- People trust AI blindly (no verification culture).
- Solutions aren't tailored to business needs (generic tools fail).
- Departments become *consumers*, not *owners* of capability.

**Solution**: Six pillars must advance together:

| Pillar | What It Does | Mining Example |
|--------|----------|---|
| **People & Mindset** | AI champions per department, growth culture, knowledge-building | Train 2–3 engineers per mine site as "AI champions" |
| **AI Companions** | Human-AI collaboration, workflow integration, not just tools | Pit planning: planners specify → AI generates scenarios → engineers verify |
| **Process & Methodology** | Adoption frameworks, change management, structured pilots | Use AISC-designed onboarding to scale across all mine sites |
| **Strategy & Leadership** | Executive sponsorship, investment commitment, clear vision | CTO/COO champions, quarterly AISC reviews |
| **Governance & Policies** | Risk management, data privacy, cost controls, responsible AI | Data governance for safety-critical decisions |
| **Tools & Platform** | Infrastructure, integrations, access, scalability | Claude Desktop for engineers, API connectors to mining systems |

### 2.3 **The AISC (AI Steering Committee) — The Enabler**

Not a decision-making bottleneck. A **coordination hub** that:
- Approves pilot projects and allocates resources
- Collects learnings and shares playbooks
- Ensures governance and risk management
- Sponsors champions and celebrates wins

For BCE: Would be composed of mine-site reps, HSSE, ops, engineering. Meets monthly. Operates 2026 enablement roadmap (Learn → Experiment → Build → Integrate → Own).

### 2.4 **Why the Middle Matters**

**The Middle-Out Insight** (Slide 11):
- Top-down (leadership) is generic, shallow, lacks execution credibility
- Bottom-up (engineers) is deep, but lacks authority to drive org change
- **Middle (champions)** has both credibility AND authority — they drive change in both directions

For mining: Shift supervisors and senior engineers, sponsored by site directors, become the **change agents**. They model AI usage, remove blockers, train peers.

### 2.5 **The Two Dimensions of AI Adoption**

Must advance together or adoption stalls:

**Individual Skill Maturity** (how people level up):
1. **Prompt Engineering**: Right questions, clear instructions
2. **Context Engineering**: Giving AI the background it needs (docs, history, tools, memory)
3. **Harness Engineering**: Building the full work environment — sandbox, verification loops, reliability

*Each level builds on the previous. Can't skip.*

**Organizational Scale** (how it spreads across the org):
1. **Perception**: Will people actually adopt it? What makes them trust/resist? (Psychology first, then tools)
2. **Change**: Does org structure actually shift? Or does it stay siloed? (Proven path: urgency → coalition → vision → wins → culture)
3. **Focus**: Where is the leverage? Top, middle, or bottom of the org? (Most orgs get this wrong — see Slide 11)

*"Individual skill without org support stays siloed. Org push without skill creates consumers, not adopters."*

### 5.5 **Slide Architecture Pattern**

Every content slide follows this template:

```
1. Background image (gradient + subtle shapes)
   ↓
2. Title (centered, Trebuchet 26pt, purple)
   ↓
3. Subtitle (centered, Century Gothic 11pt, keywords in color)
   ↓
4. Content area (1.02–5.35"):
   - Cards/rows with left accent lines
   - OR hero image with text overlay
   - OR mixed (image left, cards right)
   ↓
5. Footer bar (5.40–5.625"):
   - Source attribution (italic, small)
   - OR phase label (right-aligned)
   - Semi-transparent white background
```

**Consistency rules** (maintained across all 20 slides):
- Purple headings only
- Accent colors applied to keywords, not backgrounds
- Card panels always have left line + semi-transparent fill
- Icons size: tiny (0.15"), small (0.22"), medium (0.30"), large (0.40")
- Fonts: Trebuchet for headers, Century Gothic for body, Calibri for attribution
- No hard shadows; use transparency for depth
